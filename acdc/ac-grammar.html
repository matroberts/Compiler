The Little Language ac

Reading for Session 3

CS 4550
Translation of Programming Languages

Description

ac is a simple arithmetic language defined by Fischer, Cytron, and LeBlanc in Chapter 2 of their textbook, Crafting a Compiler. In Week 2, we study ac as the source language for a simpler compiler.

ac stands for "adding calculator" and has only a few features:
  • two data types, integer and float. Floating-point values allow five digits after the decimal point.
  • three operators, =, +, and -, with their usual meanings.
  • three reserved words:
    • f declares a float variable
    • i declares an integer variable
    • p prints its argument
  • 23 legal identifiers: the other 23 letters of the alphabet. Every identifier must be declared with its type before use.
  • implicit conversion from integers to floats, but not vice versa.


The Grammar

Here is the grammar of the ac language: 

               <program> ::= <declarations> <statements>

          <declarations> ::= <declaration> <declarations>
                           | ε

           <declaration> ::= <float-declaration> <identifier>
                           | <integer-declaration> <identifier>

            <statements> ::= <statement> <statements>
                           | ε

             <statement> ::= <identifier> <assign> <value> <expression-tail>
                           | <print> <identifier>

                 <value> ::= <identifier>
                           | <number>

       <expression-tail> ::= <plus> <value> <expression-tail>
                           | <minus> <value> <expression-tail>
                           | ε

The Ø indicates nothing, a null expression. <float-declaration>, <integer-declaration>, <assign>, <plus>, <minus>, <identifier>, and <number> are terminals that correspond to the items described informally above.
